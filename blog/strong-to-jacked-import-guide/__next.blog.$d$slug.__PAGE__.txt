e:Tf43,<p>Strong exports are valuable because they contain years of decisions: what you trained, what you lifted, how often you repeated a movement, and where progress stalled.</p>
<p>Do not treat that CSV like a disposable transfer file. Treat it like your old logbook.</p>
<p>Strong is also a common option in <a href="/blog/alternatives-to-rp-hypertrophy-app">RP Hypertrophy App alternatives</a> comparisons. If you are leaving a structured programming app for a faster logbook, import quality determines whether your old performance history remains useful.</p>
<h2 id="the-fast-path">The Fast Path</h2>
<ol>
<li>Export your workout history from Strong.</li>
<li>Save the original file somewhere safe.</li>
<li>Open the <a href="/tools/strong-csv-import-checker">Strong CSV Import Checker</a>.</li>
<li>Paste or upload a duplicate of the CSV.</li>
<li>Fix blockers before importing.</li>
<li>Import into Jacked.</li>
<li>Spot-check your main exercises before the first workout.</li>
</ol>
<p>If you only check one thing, check exercise names. Broken exercise mapping is the fastest way to make old progress hard to read.</p>
<h2 id="what-the-csv-needs">What the CSV Needs</h2>
<p>A clean lifting-history CSV should have enough structure to rebuild training history:</p>
<ul>
<li>Date</li>
<li>Workout name</li>
<li>Exercise name</li>
<li>Set order</li>
<li>Weight</li>
<li>Reps</li>
<li>Notes where available</li>
<li>Effort fields such as RPE or RIR where available</li>
</ul>
<p>Timed sets, distance rows, assisted bodyweight work, and custom exercises can still import, but they need more review.</p>
<h2 id="common-strong-csv-problems">Common Strong CSV Problems</h2>
<h3 id="missing-exercise-names">Missing Exercise Names</h3>
<p>This is a blocker. If a row has sets and load but no exercise name, the app cannot reliably attach that performance to a lift.</p>
<p>Use the <a href="/tools/workout-csv-validator">Workout CSV Validator</a> to catch blank exercise rows before import.</p>
<h3 id="mixed-units">Mixed Units</h3>
<p>Some lifters have years of mixed kg and lb history. If the export does not make units obvious, review the file before import.</p>
<p>A wrong unit can turn a normal set into a fake lifetime PR.</p>
<h3 id="custom-exercises">Custom Exercises</h3>
<p>Custom exercises are often the most important part of a long-running log. They may represent your exact machine, grip, bench angle, or rehab variation.</p>
<p>After import, check the exercises you created yourself first.</p>
<h3 id="notes-without-context">Notes Without Context</h3>
<p>Notes are useful only when they stay attached to the right workout, exercise, or set.</p>
<p>If notes shift rows during editing, they become noise. Avoid spreadsheet edits unless you know exactly what changed.</p>
<h2 id="why-jacked-cares-about-import-quality">Why Jacked Cares About Import Quality</h2>
<p>Jacked is not just storing a list of old workouts.</p>
<p>The app is built around live training decisions: next-set targets, RIR, rest timing, warm-ups, PRs, and progress review. Bad import data weakens those decisions.</p>
<p>Clean history lets Jacked answer better questions:</p>
<ul>
<li>What did you do last time?</li>
<li>Was the set hard enough?</li>
<li>Should you add load or reps?</li>
<li>Is this lift moving or stalled?</li>
<li>Is weekly volume helping or just accumulating?</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="first-workout-after-import">First Workout After Import</h2>
<p>Start conservative.</p>
<p>Use imported history as context, not as a command. If a target looks too aggressive, hold load and log a clean set with RIR. After one or two sessions, the imported history and new Jacked data will line up better.</p>
<h2 id="bottom-line">Bottom Line</h2>
<p>Do not switch apps blind.</p>
<p>Validate the Strong CSV, keep the original export, import into Jacked, and verify the lifts that matter most before training from the new history.</p>
