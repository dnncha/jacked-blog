e:T1bde,<p>Most iPhone workout apps can store exercises, sets, reps, and weights. That is useful, but it is not enough if the goal is hypertrophy.</p>
<p>A hypertrophy app should help answer the question that matters before the next set and the next session:</p>
<p><strong>What should I do now, based on what I have actually done?</strong></p>
<p>That is a different job from generic workout tracking. A good hypertrophy app should reduce friction in the gym, preserve training history, and make progressive overload easier to apply consistently.</p>
<h2 id="quick-answer">Quick Answer</h2>
<p>The best hypertrophy app for iOS should include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Fast set logging that works while you are training</li>
<li>Previous-set context and next-target suggestions</li>
<li>Rep-range and RIR-aware progression</li>
<li>Rest timing and active workout resume</li>
<li>Weekly set volume by muscle group</li>
<li>Personal records and strength trend review</li>
<li>Body weight, measurements, and progress-photo context</li>
<li>A migration path from old workout logs such as Hevy</li>
</ul>
<p>Jacked is built around that workflow: plan the session, log the set quickly, apply progression guidance, and review the result in Progress.</p>
<p>If you are specifically comparing <a href="/blog/alternatives-to-rp-hypertrophy-app">alternatives to RP Hypertrophy App</a>, the main question is whether you want the app to run a full mesocycle or help you execute your own hypertrophy training with less friction. The dedicated RP alternatives guide compares Jacked, Mesostrength, Hevy, Strong, RepLog, Liftosaur, Alpha Progression, Fitbod, Dr. Muscle, and spreadsheets by switching reason.</p>
<h2 id="what-makes-a-hypertrophy-app-different">What Makes a Hypertrophy App Different?</h2>
<p>Hypertrophy training has a few recurring problems that a generic tracker often leaves to the lifter:</p>
<ol>
<li>You need enough hard weekly sets per muscle, but not so much that performance falls apart.</li>
<li>You need progression when performance justifies it.</li>
<li>You need effort context, because 10 reps with 4 reps in reserve is different from 10 reps near failure.</li>
<li>You need consistency across weeks, not just a nice-looking session summary.</li>
</ol>
<p>That means the app needs to understand more than "weight x reps." It should support target rep ranges, RIR or effort, rest timing, history, and progress review.</p>
<h2 id="evaluation-criteria">Evaluation Criteria</h2>
<p>Use this checklist when comparing iPhone hypertrophy apps.</p>
<h3 id="1-in-gym-speed">1. In-Gym Speed</h3>
<p>The app should be fast enough that you do not avoid using it mid-workout. Useful signals include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Large tap targets</li>
<li>Previous set values prefilled or visible</li>
<li>Quick weight and rep edits</li>
<li>Rest timer that does not bury the set log</li>
<li>Undo for accidental taps</li>
</ul>
<p>If logging feels like paperwork, the data quality will collapse.</p>
<h3 id="2-progression-guidance">2. Progression Guidance</h3>
<p>Progressive overload is not just "add weight every week." A useful app should account for:</p>
<ul>
<li>Whether you hit the target rep range</li>
<li>How close to failure the set was</li>
<li>Whether the lift repeated enough times to trust the trend</li>
<li>Whether a smaller load jump or rep target is more appropriate</li>
</ul>
<p>Jacked uses the live workout context to surface practical next-load guidance rather than making the user open a spreadsheet.</p>
<p>If you want the underlying progression logic, read how a <a href="/blog/progressive-overload-app-works">progressive overload app</a> should translate recent performance into the next set.</p>
<h3 id="3-weekly-muscle-volume">3. Weekly Muscle Volume</h3>
<p>Hypertrophy depends heavily on training dose. A good app should show weekly sets by muscle group and make it clear where you are under target, in range, or overreaching.</p>
<p>This matters because a lifter can have a "good workout" that still leaves side delts, hamstrings, or calves undertrained across the week.</p>
<p>You can sanity-check this outside the app with the free <a href="/tools/weekly-volume-checker">weekly volume checker</a>.</p>
<h3 id="4-effort-and-rir-support">4. Effort and RIR Support</h3>
<p>RIR means "reps in reserve." It is an effort estimate: how many more reps you think you could have performed.</p>
<p>For hypertrophy, effort context helps distinguish easy volume from hard sets. An app does not need to force RIR on every user, but it should support it well for lifters who use autoregulation.</p>
<h3 id="5-history-import">5. History Import</h3>
<p>If you are switching from Hevy or another tracker, your old logbook should not become dead data. Imported workouts, routines, measurements, exercise notes, and set-type context help the new app understand where you are starting from.</p>
<h2 id="where-jacked-fits">Where Jacked Fits</h2>
<p>Jacked is not trying to be a general fitness app for every possible goal. It is focused on hypertrophy training on iPhone.</p>
<p>Current product strengths include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Today view for starting or resuming the right workout</li>
<li>Train view for live set logging, rest timing, RIR, and overload suggestions</li>
<li>Plan view for training days and program structure</li>
<li>Progress view for PRs, weekly muscle sets, history, photos, weigh-ins, and tape measurements</li>
<li>Hevy import for workouts, routines, measurements, notes, and historical context</li>
</ul>
<p>That combination is the important point. The app is not just storing workouts; it is trying to keep the current session and the long-term logbook connected.</p>
<h2 id="what-to-avoid">What to Avoid</h2>
<p>Be cautious with any app that:</p>
<ul>
<li>Only repeats last session's numbers without guidance</li>
<li>Hides progression behind manual spreadsheet work</li>
<li>Has no weekly muscle-volume view</li>
<li>Makes active workout resume unreliable</li>
<li>Treats progress photos and measurements as unrelated settings data</li>
<li>Cannot preserve old history when you migrate</li>
</ul>
<p>These weaknesses do not always show up in screenshots. They show up after three or four weeks, when you need the app to make the next training decision easier.</p>
<h2 id="bottom-line">Bottom Line</h2>
<p>The best hypertrophy app for iOS is the one that makes good training behavior repeatable: clear targets, fast logging, effort context, rest timing, progression guidance, and useful progress review.</p>
<p>If you care about hypertrophy more than general habit tracking, choose the app that is built around the live workout and the training history behind it.</p>
