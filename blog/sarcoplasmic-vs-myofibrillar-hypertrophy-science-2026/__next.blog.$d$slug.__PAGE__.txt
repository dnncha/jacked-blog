e:T142d,<p>You've probably heard it in every gym: <em>"Bodybuilders are bigger but weaker because they train for sarcoplasmic hypertrophy, bro."</em> It's one of fitness's most persistent myths—but what does the science actually say?</p>
<h2 id="the-two-types-of-muscle-growth-theory">The Two Types of Muscle Growth (Theory)</h2>
<p><strong>Myofibrillar hypertrophy</strong> is the growth of the contractile apparatus—the actual muscle fibers (myofibrils) that generate force. Think of these as the motors inside your muscle cells.</p>
<p><strong>Sarcoplasmic hypertrophy</strong> theoretically refers to growth of the sarcoplasm (the fluid and non-contractile components surrounding the myofibrils)—glycogen stores, mitochondria, enzymes, and sarcoplasmic proteins.</p>
<p>The claim: High-rep "pump" training increases sarcoplasm (making muscles bigger but not stronger), while heavy lifting builds myofibrils (making muscles stronger but not as big).</p>
<h2 id="what-2025-research-actually-shows">What 2025 Research Actually Shows</h2>
<p>A November 2025 study published in <em>Sport Sciences for Health</em> directly compared heavy load (myofibrillar) vs. high repetition (sarcoplasmic) training protocols. The results? Both produced significant hypertrophy, but with nuanced differences in where the growth occurred.</p>
<p>The prevailing scientific consensus has shifted:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Sarcoplasmic hypertrophy DOES happen</strong> — but probably not in the way the "bro scientists" describe it</li>
<li><strong>It's not just glycogen storage</strong> — research shows the sarcoplasm expands through increased sarcoplasmic <strong>protein content</strong>, not just fluid</li>
<li><strong>It may be a consequence, not a choice</strong> — you likely can't "target" sarcoplasmic hypertrophy specifically</li>
</ol>
<h2 id="the-real-picture">The Real Picture</h2>
<p>From PMC research (2020-2025):</p>
<blockquote>
<p><em>"If an individual exhibits a 20% increase in mean fiber cross-sectional area, and assuming myofibrils constitute ~85% of intracellular space, a 17% addition of myofibrillar protein and a 3% increase in sarcoplasm volume would accompany fiber growth."</em></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Translation: When muscles grow, <strong>both</strong> compartments expand. The sarcoplasm grows too—but at a slower rate than the myofibrils.</p>
<p>However, some evidence suggests that training history influences the ratio:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>High-rep training</strong> may favor relatively more sarcoplasmic expansion</li>
<li><strong>Heavy training</strong> may favor relatively more myofibrillar growth</li>
<li>The difference is small (~10-15% of total growth), not a complete transformation</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="why-bodybuilders-look-different">Why Bodybuilders Look Different</h2>
<p>The strength difference between bodybuilders and powerlifters isn't because bodybuilders have "non-functional" sarcoplasmic hypertrophy. It's because:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Neural adaptation</strong> — Heavy lifters practice specific movements</li>
<li><strong>Skill specificity</strong> — Strength is pattern-specific</li>
<li><strong>Muscle architecture</strong> — Bodybuilders may develop longer muscle bellies</li>
</ol>
<p>When bodybuilders switch to powerlifting, their strength skyrockets quickly—Stan Efferding squatted 900+ months after transitioning from bodybuilding. The muscle was always capable; it just needed neural adaptation.</p>
<h2 id="practical-takeaways">Practical Takeaways</h2>
<ol>
<li>
<p><strong>Don't chase "sarcoplasmic hypertrophy" specifically</strong> — Just train for muscle growth; your body will figure out the optimal ratio</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Both heavy and light training build muscle</strong> — The key driver is <strong>proximity to failure</strong>, not load</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>The pump feels real</strong> — Sarcoplasmic expansion does occur, just not in the dramatic "non-functional" way claimed</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Periodize both</strong> — Use heavy loads for strength, moderate loads for hypertrophy, and occasionally high-rep pump work for variety and metabolic stress</p>
</li>
</ol>
<h2 id="the-verdict">The Verdict</h2>
<p>Sarcoplasmic hypertrophy is real—it just isn't the magical "bigger but weaker" phenomenon described in gym mythology. When your muscles grow, <strong>both</strong> the contractile machinery and the surrounding fluid/protein matrix expand. The ratio may be slightly influenced by training style, but you can't selectively train one type over the other.</p>
<p>Stop worrying about which "type" you're building. Focus on progressive overload, eating in a surplus, training close to failure, and letting your muscles adapt naturally.</p>
